The gap

The hardest part of treatment
happens at home.

A patient finishes an infusion and goes home. Nausea peaks on day three. Fatigue settles by day five. Neuropathy builds quietly across cycles. The next appointment is two or three weeks away. In that time, symptoms appear, change, sometimes become serious. Some get mentioned at the next visit, retrospectively and from memory. Many are forgotten, minimised, or never reported at all.

50–70%
of treatment toxicities are under-reported or reported too late to act on
14–21d
between visits, depending on the regimen. Two to three weeks of clinical silence.
5mo
overall survival benefit when patients report symptoms electronically between visits

Basch et al., JAMA 2016; Basch et al., JCO 2017; Di Maio et al., NEJM 2022.

We know that systematic patient-reported outcome collection improves symptom detection, reduces emergency admissions, and extends survival. The evidence is clear. Electronic PRO platforms with structured questionnaires (PRO-CTCAE, ESAS, ePRO systems) have proven this. But adherence drops over time. The forms feel clinical, repetitive, disconnected from the patient’s actual day. Completion rates fall as treatment fatigue rises, precisely when the data matters most. Paper diaries go unfilled. Telephone triage is overwhelmed. The result is a persistent gap between what the patient experiences and what the care team knows.

Solution

Silver lives in
that gap.

Three senses, a calendar, and a clinician. Silver accompanies the patient every day, notices what the care team can’t, and hands over the moment a specialist is needed.

Foundation

Seven principles
older than software.

Silver inherits from architects and designers who understood that space, light and silence are also medicine.

Silver draws on principles from architecture and perception research: spaces that adapt to who arrives (Alexander), interfaces the body understands before the mind parses (Norman), colour as relationship not decoration (Albers), the effort of existing as a clinical variable (Sternberg), and silence as a generous act (Zumthor). The design is documented separately.

Two sides, one relationship

The AI carries the day.
The clinician carries the decisions.

Silver is never autonomous. It handles the routine: the daily check-ins, the symptom tracking, the quiet questions at 2 a.m. But the moment something matters, a real clinician is there. The patient is always in a relationship with a person. Silver just makes sure that person has the full picture when they arrive.

Voice architecture

How the companion thinks.

A multi-layered contextual prompt system tailored to each patient. Every layer is a filter. Every filter is a promise: the patient will not be reduced.

Every response is shaped by the treatment protocol, the patient's history, where they are in the cycle, how they feel today, and what they said last time. Nothing is generic.

Voice

The patient speaks naturally, in their own language. Speech is transcribed in real time, the agent responds in text and voice simultaneously. Sub-second latency on transcription, warm synthetic voice on output.

Behavioural engine

The agent adapts its posture on a continuous spectrum: quiet and protective on bad days, curious and expansive on good ones. Driven by mood, symptom load, treatment cycle day and wearable recovery score.

Patient surface

Mobile-first, designed for native deployment. The orb is the primary interface. Voice is the primary input. Text is a fallback. Wearable data flows in silently through a health bridge.

Safety layer

Every utterance passes through crisis detection, clinical scope checks and red-flag extraction before reaching the patient. When something crosses a threshold, the care team is notified with full clinical context.

Mapped toxicity profiles

Each treatment protocol carries a mapped toxicity profile. The system knows the expected side effects for the regimen, when they typically appear, and how they evolve across cycles.

Nadir = emergency awareness

During the nadir window the system elevates its clinical vigilance. Fever is treated as a potential emergency. The patient is told to go to the hospital. The care team is alerted.

Proactive outreach triggers

Four calendar-driven triggers: nadir_start (high priority, immune warning), pre_cycle (eating, hydration advice), post_acute (day 4 check-in), recovery_start (post-nadir encouragement). Silver reaches out, it does not only respond.

72-hour silence detection

When the patient goes quiet for more than 72 hours, Silver reaches out. During a nadir window, silence is a clinical signal. Outside it, a gentle check-in.

Configurable regimen profiles

Each treatment regimen is modelled with its own cycle length, nadir window, expected toxicity timeline and onset patterns. New regimens can be added as structured profiles. The system ships with common chemotherapy and immunotherapy schemes and is designed to grow with the institution.

Phase-aware prompt context

Every response is informed by the current phase (acute, developing, nadir, recovery), the expected toxicities for this day, and whether the patient is in a known danger window.

Silence as a signal

If the patient goes quiet during a crash window, that’s expected. If they go quiet during a stable week, that’s a signal worth flagging.

Clinical substrate

The companion adapts.

The companion adapts its tone and depth to good days and bad days. On bad days it is quiet, protective, low-burden. On good days it is curious, expansive, willing to go deeper. The transition is continuous, driven by symptom load, emotional state, and wearable recovery data.

Low bandwidth mode

Simpler questions, larger type, fewer affordances. Recovery score front and centre. Symptoms inferred silently from minimal input.

High bandwidth mode

Full conversation. Multi-symptom exploration. Trend review. Treatment calendar awareness.

Wellness scoring

A composite readiness indicator drawn from wearable data, mood history, and symptom burden. This is a proprietary wellness metric, not a validated clinical measure. It should not be used for treatment decisions.

9 mood states

Hopeful, calm, energized, neutral, anxious, sad, pain, distress, angry. Each mapped to a numeric value. Trend calculated from mood history. Feeds into wellness score and UI palette.

CTCAE v5 · inferred from conversation

CTCAE-informed symptom characterisation, under active validation. Severity is inferred from natural conversation about onset, frequency and interference. Not a validated diagnostic tool. Clinical decisions remain with the treating physician.

Three-layer memory

Turn memory (ephemeral per-turn facts), symptom memory (per-symptom fact history across turns), encounter memory (full encounter summary). Each layer has different scope and lifecycle. Full encounter persistence in SQLite.

Clinical intelligence

How Silver thinks.

Not a generic chatbot. Silver follows protocol-driven clinical flows, tracks multiple symptoms simultaneously, uses structured function calling, and renders questions without LLM calls when it can.

Multi-symptom tracking

Silver tracks multiple symptoms in parallel, each with its own clinical history. When a combination of symptoms suggests something serious, the system flags it.

19 symptom classes

A structured clinical taxonomy: fever, general, GI (constipation, diarrhea, nausea/vomiting, other), neuro (dizziness, other), pain (abdomen, chest, general, head), psych, respiratory (cough, dyspnea, other), skin (rash/itch), urinary (hematuria, other). Each class maps to a protocol-defined assessment pathway.

Protocol-driven flow navigation

The clinical assessment follows structured pathways, not improvised conversation. Each symptom type has a defined sequence of questions — onset, frequency, severity, interference — that the agent follows consistently.

Question rendering without LLM

Standard clinical questions are rendered from templates, not generated. This means consistent phrasing, lower latency, and no risk of hallucinated questions for routine assessments.

Five agent tools

The AI uses structured function calling to classify symptoms, fetch the right assessment questions, extract clinical facts from free text, evaluate severity, and advance the conversation. The reasoning and the execution are separated.

Medical term clarification

A built-in dictionary of medical terms with plain-language explanations in the patient's language. When a patient asks what a term means, the agent explains immediately with a verified definition.

Body storytelling

Patients see stories, not numbers. “You had an active day, more steps than last week.” Clinicians see the raw data. Same information, different language.

Extraction schema

On every patient message, clinical facts are extracted in parallel with the conversation — symptoms identified, severity assessed, patterns flagged. The patient never waits for this process.

Encounter context with rollback

Every turn is processed safely. If any part of the pipeline fails, the system recovers gracefully. The patient never sees a broken or half-processed response.

Clinician oversight

The moment a
clinician steps in.

Silver does most of the everyday work alone: the gentle check-ins, the symptom tracking, the questions about treatment. But the AI knows where its edges are. A five-level escalation system runs on every encounter. Every agent output passes through safety checks before reaching the patient. When something crosses a line, Silver acts with full context via a structured clinical handoff to the care team.

IMMEDIATE · 0 min · red flags / CTCAE G4–5

Patient directed to hospital.

When the system detects a potentially life-threatening adverse event — based on predefined clinical criteria reviewed by the supervising oncologist — it instructs the patient to seek emergency care immediately, in their own language, and simultaneously alerts the care team with full clinical context.

  • Immediate, unambiguous instruction to the patient
  • Structured clinical handoff to the oncology service
  • Companion stays with the patient through the transition
  • Encounter tagged as IMMEDIATE · hospital-directed in the audit trail

Triage thresholds follow published oncology emergency guidelines. The specific criteria for each condition are defined in the clinical protocol and reviewed by the supervising oncologist.

URGENT · 1h · CTCAE G3

Clinician notified within the hour.

Grade 3 symptoms that don’t demand the ER but do demand attention. Silver pushes a structured notification with the symptom, the trend, the wearable context, the cycle day, and the AI’s recommended next step. The clinician decides.

HIGH · 4h

Worsening trends, multiple grade 2 symptoms, or concerning wearable patterns. The clinician sees it in their next check-in window.

MODERATE · 24h

Isolated grade 2 symptoms with stable trends. Flagged for next-day review. Patient reassured but monitored.

LOW · 72h

Grade 1 observations, minor concerns, or lifestyle data that may inform the next consultation. Logged, not pushed.

Crisis detection

Designed to flag acute psychological distress for clinical review. Every encounter is screened, graded by severity, and escalated to the care team when a critical signal is detected.

Drug interactions

Designed to surface relevant symptom context for the clinician. Flagged concerns are routed for review. This is a safety net, not a substitute for pharmacist oversight.

Per-turn output guardrails

Every response is checked before reaching the patient. Out-of-scope content, unsafe recommendations, and low-quality outputs are caught and filtered automatically.

Structured handoff

Every escalation generates a structured clinical handoff to the care team, including relevant context, symptom data, and a suggested next step.

Full context on handoff

The clinician opens Silver and sees the last seven days of signals, symptoms and transcripts. Not a blank screen.

The AI stays present

Escalation doesn’t end the companion. It keeps the patient company while the clinician approaches.

Every handoff logged

Audit trail of what the AI saw, what it decided, and when the clinician took over. Transparency by default.

Data governance

Built for regulated health data.

Silver processes sensitive health data in compliance with applicable privacy frameworks — GDPR in Europe, HIPAA in the US, LGPD in Brazil, and equivalent regulations worldwide. Voice recordings, wearable signals and clinical symptoms are stored encrypted, with explicit patient consent, and retained only for the duration of the care relationship. Patients can request access, correction or deletion at any time. Data residency is configurable per deployment.

Consent

Explicit informed consent before any data collection. Patients can withdraw at any time without affecting their care.

Storage

Data residency follows local regulation. Encryption at rest, transmitted over TLS. Retention limited to the active care period plus the legally required archive.

Access rights

Patients can view, export, correct or delete their data. Clinicians access only their own patients. Audit trail on every access.
